This is an honest look at where the church stands — nationally, and right here. Across America the familiar form of church has thinned. But this isn't a session about decline for its own sake. It's about a single rural congregation, rooted in one place, asking a real question: given everything that has changed in our culture, our politics, and our technology, where do we go next? Not back to a remembered peak, but forward into a landscape that is genuinely new. What follows is the ground we walk before we sit down together to discern it.

The honest posture holds two truths at once — the decline here is real, and the peak we remember was never the whole story, nor is it coming back.

What changed? First, we stopped joining — everything. For most of the twentieth century, churches were part of America's social infrastructure, not merely places of belief but the connective tissue of community life, alongside the lodges, leagues, and clubs that Robert Putnam catalogued in Bowling Alone. As those civic habits frayed and Americans withdrew from collective life across the board, churchgoing fell with the tide. The decline of the church is, in part, a chapter in a much larger story: a whole culture slowly forgetting how to belong to anything together.

Then the numbers. In 2020, US church membership dropped below fifty percent for the first time ever, down from about seventy percent in 1999. Christian identity fell from 78 percent in 2007 to 62 percent, while the religiously unaffiliated — the "nones" — nearly doubled to around 29 percent. Many still claim a church while attending monthly and doing little — belonging itself, not just belief, has thinned.

What's actually "coming back" isn't the traditional church — it's the form that fits the culture, and mostly that means the megachurch. Think small business versus big-box retail: the consumer form scales, markets, and absorbs, while the corner store struggles to compete on those terms. That's not a partisan point; it's a description of a market. The culturally-matched form survives because it plays the culture's game.

How did Jesus grow a movement? It's worth sitting with the question before reaching for an answer, because the honest answer cuts against every instinct the content age trains into us. He didn't grow it through reach, production, or spectacle. The mechanism was startlingly low-tech — and it's the same mechanism still available to the smallest church in the most out-of-the-way place. The way the movement actually spread is not a strategy we have to invent; it's one we only have to recover.
