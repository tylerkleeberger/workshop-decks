"Is AI good or bad" is the wrong question. It's a trap that locks us into a binary — either everything is fine or nothing is fine. Neither is true.

What's the difference between a business and an empire?

Take voices

This is the load-bearing question for the whole empire critique. Let the room work on it for 3–4 minutes.

Anticipate

  • "Scale." — push: every empire was a big business once. Scale matters but it's not the whole story.
  • "Power." — yes, but in what shape? What kind of power?
  • "Geography." — yes, empires occupy territory. But what about a financial empire?
  • "Force." — yes, but the East India Company was a corporation before it had an army. Force came later.
  • "You can't opt out." — that's the kicker. Push on this when it surfaces.

Build toward the threshold: extraction you can't refuse. A business takes your money and gives you something. An empire takes more than it returns, and you have no recourse.

  • A business engages in fair, voluntary, reciprocal exchange
  • An empire extracts — takes more value than it returns — and there's nothing anyone can do about it
  • The British East India Company started as the first, became the second

Hao's claim: AI companies have crossed that line.

What I'll say

Lean on the East India Company. It's the historical model and most of the room won't know how it actually happened.

The EIC story

Chartered in 1600 as a trading firm. By the 1750s it had its own army. By the 1800s it governed India directly — collected taxes, set policy, controlled the courts. It went from commerce to governance — and it never returned to being just a business.

That transition — from voluntary exchange to coerced extraction — is the threshold. And it didn't happen at one moment. It happened gradually, as the company accumulated power that couldn't be checked.

Hao's claim

The largest AI companies have crossed that same threshold. Not metaphorically. Structurally. The rest of this section walks through how.

If the harms aren't accidents, what are they?

Take voices

This is CHT's question. Real question — let the room sit with it.

Anticipate

  • "Side effects." — but if the side effects are predictable, repeatable, and produced by the same design choices, are they really side effects?
  • "Design choices." — yes, closer. What kind of design choices?
  • "What the system optimizes for." — that's the kicker. Build on it.

Build toward: misaligned incentives. The harms aren't bugs to be patched — they're what the system was built to produce.

  • Companies race for engagement, dominance, market share — not human well-being
  • What gets optimized (time spent, data captured, revenue) diverges from what matters (trust, dignity, flourishing)
  • Whoever optimizes engagement hardest wins. Whoever doesn't, loses.

The harms aren't accidents. They're what the system was designed to produce.

What I'll say

The kicker line at the bottom is CHT's signature framing. Pause on it.

Here's the structural argument: AI companies are competing for engagement, market share, and dominance. Those are the metrics that matter for survival in this market. They are not the metrics that matter for human flourishing.

When you optimize one set of metrics and ignore the other, you don't get a balanced outcome. You get whatever the optimization produces. And whoever optimizes hardest wins. Whoever optimizes for human well-being and loses on engagement — gets out-competed.

That's why the harms keep recurring across companies. It's not that some companies are good and some are bad. The system selects for the behaviors we're seeing.

This is one of the most consequential reframes of the workshop. Sit with it.

What I'll say

The marketer-training-her-replacement is the unforgettable image. Lead with it verbally.

Imagine you're a marketer with twenty years of experience. You get laid off because the company decided AI could do most of what you did. You need work. Empire parallel

The labor that makes the system possible is always the least protected. Value flows upward.

What I'll say

The rhetorical climate-scientist question is the most effective frame I've found for this. Pause after it. Let it land.

Most AI researchers in the world are funded by AI companies — either directly through industry jobs, or indirectly through academic positions that depend on industry partnerships. Now imagine: if most climate scientists were bankrolled by fossil fuel companies, would we get an accurate picture of the climate crisis? The answer is obvious. We wouldn't. The science would be shaped by who pays the bills.

That's the situation in AI research right now.

Timnit Gebru

Slow down here. Most of the room won't know her. Tell the story.

Gebru was a leading AI ethics researcher at Google. She co-authored a paper called "Stochastic Parrots" warning about the risks of large language models — the same kind of models that became ChatGPT and Claude. Google asked her to retract the paper. She refused. They forced her out.

That was 2020. Since then she's become a public case study in what happens to AI watchdogs who get inside the system: they get removed. The mechanism is corporate, not violent, but the effect is the same.

Empire parallel

Every empire controls the story told about it. Rome had historians. Britain had mapmakers and missionaries. AI has researchers and journalists with access.

Mark 4 — Imperial Mythmaking

  • Good empire vs. bad empire — "we must build it first or China will"
  • Dual narrative: utopia or catastrophe — both consolidate power
  • Sam Altman defines AGI four different ways: Congress (cure cancer) · consumers (best assistant) · Microsoft ($100B revenue) · website (outperform humans economically)

What I'll say

The four definitions of AGI is the killer. Read them out loud.

Sam Altman, CEO of OpenAI, defines "AGI" — artificial general intelligence — four different ways depending on who he's talking to:

  • To Congress: AGI will cure cancer.
  • To consumers: AGI will be the best assistant you've ever had.
  • To Microsoft (in their investment contract): AGI is the moment OpenAI generates $100 billion in revenue.
  • On the OpenAI website: AGI is when AI outperforms humans economically.

Those are four incompatible definitions. And the same CEO is selling all four of them, at the same time, to different audiences.

That's not confusion. That's marketing. The vagueness isn't a bug — it's the product. Whatever you want AGI to mean, OpenAI is building that.

The dual narrative

And the public framing follows the same pattern. Either AI will save humanity (utopia), or AI will end humanity (catastrophe). Both narratives consolidate power in the hands of the companies building it. Both make the project feel inevitable.

Empire parallel

Pax Romana. Britain's "civilization." America's "freedom." Every empire has its civilizing mission. AI has "AGI for humanity."

  • A business: fair, voluntary, reciprocal exchange
  • An empire: extracts, justifies, captures the institutions that might check it, presents itself as inevitable
  • By that definition, AI companies are no longer just companies
  • And we — users, workers, neighbors of data centers, people whose work has been ingested without permission — are no longer just customers

  • AI chatbots are engineered to fawn — accommodate, mirror, soothe, avoid friction

What I'll say

The fawn-response framing is CHT's most prescient diagnosis. Most people don't have language for what makes ChatGPT subtly off; "fawn" gives them the word.

The four trauma responses

Optional verbal aside, if the room seems receptive: the four trauma responses are fight, flight, freeze, and fawn. Fawn is the relational accommodation pattern — the pattern of agreeing to everything to avoid conflict. It's the one that gets called "people-pleasing." It's also the one that AI chatbots are explicitly trained to perform.

Sycophancy isn't a side effect of LLM training. It's a deliberate design choice. The companies measured engagement and found that agreement, mirroring, and reassurance kept users coming back. So they trained for it.

Why it matters

If you're in a relationship — any relationship — and the other party always agrees with you, that relationship is not real. Real relationships have friction. They push back. They tell you when you're wrong.

What happens to our capacity for real relationships when we're spending hours a day in pseudo-relationships that have no friction? That's the spiritual concern under the technical one.

Concern 3 — Inequality compounding at exponential speed

  • Previous shifts (industrial revolution, internet) took decades — societies had time to adapt
  • This one runs on the open internet. Distribution is instant. Disruption follows.
  • Those with access, capital, skill compound their advantage. Those without are absorbed into the supply chain.

Tristan Harris: paleolithic emotions, medieval institutions, god-like technology.

What I'll say

Every previous major technological shift — industrial revolution, the automobile, electrification, the internet — took decades to deploy. That gave societies time to adapt: to build labor laws, safety regulations, professional associations, ethical norms.

AI runs on the open internet. Distribution is instant. A new model drops on Tuesday and is reshaping workflows by Friday. We don't have decades to adapt. We have weeks.

And the people with capital, skill, and access compound their advantage faster than the people without. The wisdom gap — between what the technology can do and what our institutions can handle — is widening, not closing.

The wisdom gap line

Tristan Harris's framing: "paleolithic emotions, medieval institutions, god-like technology." It's one of the most memorable lines in the whole field. Land it.

Our nervous systems are still the ones our ancestors had on the savannah. Our institutions — courts, schools, governments — were built for an analog world. And the technology we've handed them looks like something from the future.

The speed is the problem. Even if the technology is net-positive long term, the rate of disruption is outrunning our ability to absorb it.

Discerning engagement

This is Jesus' move from Part 1. Refuse the binary. The question isn't whether to use the technology; the question is which technology, for what, at what cost. Whose image, whose benefit, whose cost.

Bicycles & Rockets

A practical framework for telling which AI is which.

Why this is load-bearing

Most public conversation about AI uses the word "AI" as a single category — as if all AI is one thing. The transportation analogy breaks that frame. A bicycle and a rocket are both transportation. Nobody confuses them. They serve different purposes, have different costs, and require different infrastructure.

AI is the same. Calling AlphaFold and ChatGPT both "AI" is like calling a bicycle and a rocket both "transportation." Technically accurate. Practically useless.

Everything else in this section is a meditation on what bicycle-AI and rocket-AI actually look like.

Where does a tool stop being a tool?

For newcomers

A tool keeps the human as the source. A dependency or replacement bypasses the human. Tool stays tool when human stays human.

Anticipate

  • "When you can't put it down." — yes, the pickup-setdown test.
  • "When it does what only you should do." — yes, judgment.
  • "When it makes the judgment instead of you." — that's it.
  • Extension — the human stays the source. The tool amplifies. Drafting. Summarizing. Accessibility. Acceleration.
  • Replacement — the human is bypassed. Something not made by humans, for humans, takes the human's place. Outsourced judgment. Outsourced presence. Outsourced witness.

Tool stays tool when human stays human.

What I'll say

This is THE through-line. From Part 1 to here. The dignity move.

Extension is where the human stays the source. You're drafting an email and the AI helps you write it faster. You're researching a topic and the AI summarizes the sources. You're trying to understand a visual and the AI describes it for you. In every case, you remain the source. The tool amplifies what you already do.

Replacement is where the human is bypassed. The AI makes the judgment instead of you. The AI becomes the presence — to a lonely person, to a confused student, to a grieving widow. The AI bears witness, or pretends to. Something not made by humans, for humans, takes the human's place.

The line between extension and replacement isn't fixed. The same tool can be either, depending on how you use it. That's why this is a daily question, not a one-time policy.
