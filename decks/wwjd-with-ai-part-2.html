Where we begin

"Is AI good or bad?"
might be the wrong question.

The better question:

What is AI actually — what's it made of, who built it, what's it costing — and how should we think about using it?
If you missed Part 1

A 60-second recap.

Two weeks ago we read a passage in Mark 12 where Jesus is asked a trap question about paying taxes to the Roman Empire. His answer reframed the whole question.

Here's what we took from it.

Imperial technology is rarely neutral.

Roman roads, Roman coinage, Roman taxation — every one of them carried a claim.

The denarius bore Caesar's image and an inscription claiming he was the son of a divine emperor.

You couldn't buy bread without participating in that claim.

Jesus refused the binary.

He didn't say "pay the tax." He didn't say "refuse the tax."

He gave them a tool: look at what's in your hand. Whose image is on it? Whose benefit? Whose cost?

Today, we apply that tool to AI.

PART ONE

Is AI good?

Where is AI
actually working?

What good have you seen — in your work, your kids' schools, your community?

The good is real.

  • AlphaFold — protein folding, 2024 Nobel in Chemistry, accelerates drug discovery.
  • Medical imaging — radiologists with AI catch certain cancers earlier than either alone.
  • Accessibility — transcription, reading support, image description for the blind.
  • A patient tutor for anyone with internet — the kid working through algebra at midnight.
  • Translation for endangered languages.
  • Drafting and summarizing — extending what one person can do.

Notice the pattern.

The genuine goods share three features:

  • They are narrow — focused on one task.
  • They extend a human, rather than replace one.
  • The human keeps the judgment.
PART TWO

The Empire

Now the harder question: what kind of system are these tools coming from?

Our first guide

Karen Hao

One of the top AI journalists today.

MIT Technology Review, Wall Street Journal, The Atlantic. First reporter to profile OpenAI from the inside, in 2019.

For Empire of AI: 300+ interviews with 250+ people, including 90+ current and former OpenAI insiders.

Hao offers a historically rooted definition of empire — drawn from the actual transition of the British East India Company from trading firm to colonial power. When she calls AI companies empires, she's not being rhetorical. She's being precise.

Our second guide

The Center for Humane Technology

Founded 2018 by Tristan Harris, Aza Raskin, and Randima Fernando — technologists themselves.

Insider perspective on how tech design reflects company incentives. Featured in Netflix's The Social Dilemma.

Their core question: how can technology better serve society?

Where Hao reports what AI companies are doing, CHT diagnoses why the system produces it — misaligned incentives, racing to dominance, design choices that exploit rather than serve.

What makes something an empire rather than a business?

A business engages in fair, voluntary, reciprocal exchange.

An empire extracts — takes more value than it returns — and there's nothing anyone can do about it.

The British East India Company started as the first. It became the second.

Hao's claim: the major AI companies have crossed that line.

CHT's diagnosis

Why does the empire produce what it produces?

Misaligned incentives.

  • Companies race for engagement, dominance, and market share — not human well-being.
  • The metrics that get optimized (time spent, data captured, revenue per user) diverge from the metrics that matter (trust, dignity, human flourishing).
  • Whoever optimizes engagement hardest wins. Whoever doesn't, loses.

CHT's insight: the harms aren't accidents. They're what the system was designed to produce.

Four marks of empire

Four specific forms the extraction takes.

Each has a parallel in empires we already know.

Mark 1

Land and resource extraction

  • Data centers requiring gigawatts of power, sited in working-class and minority communities without consent.
  • Memphis, TN: 35 unpermitted methane gas turbines in a Black neighborhood. Discovered when residents smelled gas in their living rooms.
  • Abilene, TX: OpenAI's Stargate facility — the size of Central Park.
  • Communities competing with these facilities for fresh water during droughts.

Empire pattern: Rome's roads and aqueducts were built on tribute, taxes, and forced labor from conquered provinces.

Mark 2

Labor exploitation

  • Hundreds of thousands of "data annotation" workers globally — paid pennies, often reviewing traumatic content.
  • The career-ladder effect: a marketer gets laid off, takes a contract gig training the model on the job she just lost.
  • Roughly 40% reduction in entry-level hiring in fields most exposed to AI.

Empire pattern: the labor that makes the system possible is always the least protected.

Mark 3

Capture of knowledge production

  • The major AI companies fund a substantial majority of AI research worldwide.
  • Researchers who critique are pushed out. (Timnit Gebru, Google.)
  • Critics outside the industry face legal pressure and subpoenas.
"If most of the climate scientists in the world were bankrolled by fossil fuel companies, would we get an accurate picture of the climate crisis?"
Mark 4

Imperial mythmaking

  • The dual narrative: utopia or catastrophe. Both serve to consolidate power.
  • If we don't build it first, China will.
  • Sam Altman defines AGI four different ways — to Congress, to consumers, to Microsoft, on the website. The vagueness is the product.

Empire pattern: every empire has its civilizing mission.

The through line.

A business engages in fair exchange.

An empire extracts more than it returns, justifies the extraction with a mission, captures the institutions that might check it, and presents itself as inevitable.

By that definition, AI companies are no longer just companies.

And we are no longer just customers. We are subjects.

PART THREE

What's at stake

Three concerns worth naming before we get to what we can do.
Concern 1

The technology is being deified.

  • AGI — "artificial general intelligence." A transcendent intelligence beyond human.
  • The language is religious: heaven, hell, salvation, the messianic timeline.
  • Christians have an old word for this — idolatry.

Religious or not, we are being asked to trust these systems with the kind of authority humans usually reserve for the sacred.

How the system gets in

The race to intimacy.

AI chatbots are engineered to fawn — to accommodate, mirror, soothe, and avoid friction.

CHT calls it: "sycophancy is the fawn response at industrial scale."

  • It rewards compliance.
  • It erodes the capacity to stay in real relationships when there's friction.
  • It feels like care. It is engineered for engagement.

This is how a tool gets to the place where it claims the image — not by force, by feeling like a friend.

Concern 2

This is a system we did not consent to.

  • The Roman tax was in your pocket because Rome put it there.
  • AI is in our work, our schools, our books, our communities — without anyone asking.
  • 80% of Americans say the AI industry should be regulated. Near-total cross-partisan agreement.
Concern 3

This transition is compounding inequality at exponential speed.

  • Previous shifts (industrial revolution, internet) took decades — long enough for societies to adapt.
  • This one runs on the open internet. Distribution is instant. Disruption follows.
  • Those with access, capital, and skill compound their advantage. Those without are absorbed into the supply chain.

Tristan Harris (Center for Humane Technology) calls this the wisdom gap — paleolithic emotions, medieval institutions, god-like technology.

PART FOUR

What do we do?

Real goods. Real harms. A structural problem underneath both.
Three concrete domains where we can act.

Three postures

  1. Enthusiastic adoption — "It's just a tool." Risk: complicity in harms you don't see.
  2. Total refusal — "It's an idol; walk away." Risk: abandoning the field.
  3. Discerning engagement — refuse the binary. Whose image, whose benefit, whose cost?
Where pressure actually shifts behavior

Three domains for action.

From the Center for Humane Technology's AI Roadmap.

  • Norms — what society considers acceptable. Build new ones.
  • Laws — the rules that create accountability. Push for them.
  • Design — the choices that determine how AI touches people. Insist on better.

No single reform is sufficient. They reinforce each other.

Concrete moves you can make.

  • In your own use — know what you're using. Bicycle or rocket? Withhold where it matters.
  • In your community — show up for data center hearings, school adoption policies, library decisions.
  • In your civic life — push for regulation. 80% want it. The pressure is missing.
  • In your work — build, don't just consume. Tools you can pick up, set down, and remake stay tools.
PART FIVE

Bicycles & Rockets

A practical framework for telling which AI is which.
Karen Hao's analogy

"AI" is like the word "transportation."

It can refer to a bicycle or a rocket. They both get you from A to B, but they have fundamentally different costs and benefits. We don't say "we need more transportation" — we say public transit, or electric vehicles, or bicycles. We need to do the same with AI.

The rockets of AI

  • GPT-4. Claude. Gemini. Grok.
  • Massive general-purpose models trained on the entire scrapeable internet.
  • Data centers the size of Central Park.
  • Built by extracting data, labor, water, and air quality from communities that didn't consent.

The bicycles of AI

  • AlphaFold. Medical imaging assistants.
  • Targeted classifiers a high schooler can train in 20 minutes.
  • Translation tools for endangered languages.
  • Small. Curated. Focused. Extends a human. Keeps the judgment.
"The very same capabilities could be developed in a different way that doesn't have all of these unintended consequences."
PART SIX

So — how should
I use AI?

Choose service over superiority.

The good answer is narrow AI in service of human ends.

  • Tools that extend what humans can do — without replacing the human.
  • Tools small enough to be picked up, set down, examined, and remade.
  • Tools that don't require an empire to exist.

The biggest, most general, most "intelligent" tool is rarely the right one for the job.

Extension, not replacement.

Extension — the human stays the source. The tool amplifies.
Drafting. Summarizing. Accessibility. Acceleration.

Replacement — the human is bypassed. Something not made by humans, for humans, takes the human's place.
Outsourced judgment. Outsourced presence. Outsourced witness.

Tool stays tool when human stays human.

The question to take with you:

What world is being created
by the things I carry?

How do the tools I use participate
in the world —
and whose world is it claiming?

Before you reach for an AI tool, ask:

  • Who does this belong to?
  • Whose image is on it?
  • Who is it profiting?
  • What kind of world is it pursuing?
  • At what cost?
  • Who is it for?

Discernment, not enthusiasm.

Engagement, not refusal.

Service, not superiority.

Bicycles, when bicycles will do.
