Technological ethics for the age of AI: tools, systems, and how to tell the difference.

Is AI Good?

Pause. Smile. The previous section set up the structural critique — this section deliberately starts somewhere else: where is AI actually working in your life?

Why this order matters: if you lead with the empire critique, people get defensive. If you lead with their own concrete experience of AI doing something useful, the critique that follows lands differently — it doesn't feel like an attack on their tools.

Where is AI actually working in your life right now?

  • Work tasks (drafting emails, summarizing reports)
  • Coding / development
  • Medical (radiology, dermatology)
  • Accessibility (transcription, image description)
  • Translation
  • Voice assistants, navigation, search

  • A patient tutor for anyone with internet

Reveal AFTER the room has surfaced 4–5 of these themselves.

What do these good uses have in common?

The Empire

Tonal shift. The previous section was about what AI does well. This section is about how the largest AI systems are built — and what they cost.

Don't preview the critique. Let the next two slides do the work of introducing the guides.

Karen Hao

MIT Technology Review · Wall Street Journal · The Atlantic

  • First reporter to profile OpenAI from the inside (2019)
  • Empire of AI: 300+ interviews, 90+ current/former OpenAI employees
  • ~7 years of reporting
30 seconds. Name her credentials briefly.

Key move: Hao offers a historically rooted definition of empire — drawn from the actual transition of the British East India Company from trading firm to colonial power. When she calls AI companies empires, she's being precise, not rhetorical.

That precision matters. Some in the room will hear "empire" as activist language. Name it as historical analysis.

Center for Humane Technology

Tristan Harris · Aza Raskin · Randima Fernando — founded 2018

  • Insider perspective on how tech design reflects company incentives
  • Featured in The Social Dilemma
  • Core question: how can technology better serve society?
30 seconds.

The pairing: Where Hao reports what AI companies are doing, CHT diagnoses why the system produces it — misaligned incentives, racing to dominance, design choices that exploit rather than serve.

Two perspectives on the same problem. Hao = the reporter; CHT = the diagnostician.

What's the difference between a business and an empire?

Real question. Take voices.

Anticipate:
  • "Scale." — push: every empire was a big business once.
  • "Power." — yes, but in what shape?
  • "Geography." — yes, empires occupy territory.
  • "Force." — yes, but the East India Company was a corporation before it had an army.
  • "You can't opt out." — that's the kicker.
Build toward the threshold: extraction you can't refuse.
  • A business engages in fair, voluntary, reciprocal exchange
  • An empire extracts — takes more value than it returns — and there's nothing anyone can do about it
  • The British East India Company started as the first, became the second

Hao's claim: AI companies have crossed that line.

Lean on the East India Company. It's the historical model and most of the room won't know how it happened.

Briefly: EIC was a trading firm chartered in 1600. By 1750s it had its own army. By 1800s it governed India. It went from commerce to governance — and never returned.

Hao's claim isn't metaphor. She's reading the transition.

If the harms aren't accidents, what are they?

This is CHT's question. Take voices.

Anticipate:
  • "Side effects." — but if the side effects are predictable, are they really side effects?
  • "Design choices." — yes, closer.
  • "What the system optimizes for." — that's the kicker.
Build toward: misaligned incentives. The harms aren't bugs; they're features.
  • Companies race for engagement, dominance, market share — not human well-being
  • What gets optimized (time spent, data captured, revenue) diverges from what matters (trust, dignity, flourishing)
  • Whoever optimizes engagement hardest wins. Whoever doesn't, loses.

The harms aren't accidents. They're what the system was designed to produce.

The kicker line at the bottom is CHT's signature framing. Pause on it.

This is one of the most consequential reframes of the workshop. Sit with it.

If AI companies are empires, what do they extract from?

Anticipate:
  • Data — yes (will land in Mark 3)
  • Labor — yes (Mark 2)
  • Energy / land — yes (Mark 1)
  • Attention — yes (touches all four)
  • Knowledge / narrative — yes (Mark 4)
Map their answers to the four marks coming. If they name all four, great — the reveals confirm. If they name two, the reveals expand.

Mark 1 — Land & Resource Extraction

  • Data centers requiring gigawatts, sited in working-class & minority communities
  • Memphis xAI Colossus — 35 unpermitted methane turbines in a Black neighborhood with high lung cancer rates
  • Abilene Stargate — "the size of Central Park"
  • Communities competing with these facilities for fresh water during droughts
Lean into specifics. The Memphis case is the strongest. The methane turbines weren't permitted; the neighborhood already had high lung cancer rates before xAI arrived.

Empire parallel: Rome's roads and aqueducts were built on tribute, taxes, and forced labor from conquered provinces. The infrastructure was funded by extraction.

If anyone in the room has heard about local data center fights (zoning, water, power) — pull them in.

Mark 2 — Labor Exploitation

  • Hundreds of thousands of data annotators globally — Kenya, Philippines, India, increasingly U.S. — pennies, often reviewing traumatic content
  • Career-ladder break: marketer laid off, takes a contract gig training the model on the job she just lost
  • ~40% reduction in entry-level hiring in fields most exposed to AI
  • LLMs trained explicitly for the industries where most profit can be made
The marketer-training-her-replacement is the unforgettable image. Lead with it verbally.

Empire parallel: the labor that makes the system possible is always the least protected. Value flows upward.

The 40% number lands hard for parents in the room. Don't soften it.

Mark 3 — Capture of Knowledge Production

  • The AI industry funds most AI researchers in the world
  • If most climate scientists were bankrolled by fossil fuel companies, would we get an accurate picture of the climate crisis?
  • Dissidents removed — Timnit Gebru at Google is the canonical case
  • Watchdogs face legal pressure (OpenAI subpoena fishing expeditions)
The rhetorical climate-scientist question lands hard. Pause after it.

Slow down on Gebru. Most in the room won't know who she is. AI ethics researcher at Google. Co-authored "Stochastic Parrots" paper warning about large language models. Forced out in 2020. Became a public case study in what happens to AI watchdogs who get inside.

Empire parallel: every empire controls the story told about it. Rome had historians. Britain had mapmakers and missionaries. AI has researchers and journalists with access.

Mark 4 — Imperial Mythmaking

  • Good empire vs. bad empire — "we must build it first or China will"
  • Dual narrative: utopia or catastrophe — both consolidate power
  • Sam Altman defines AGI four different ways: Congress (cure cancer) · consumers (best assistant) · Microsoft ($100B revenue) · website (outperform humans economically)
  • Vagueness isn't a bug. It's the product.
The four definitions of AGI is the killer. Read them out loud.

Let the absurdity land. The same CEO is selling four incompatible futures to four audiences, and we're supposed to call that a coherent technology.

Empire parallel: Pax Romana. Britain's "civilization." America's "freedom." Every empire has its civilizing mission. AI has "AGI for humanity."
  • A business: fair, voluntary, reciprocal exchange
  • An empire: extracts, justifies, captures the institutions that might check it, presents itself as inevitable
  • By that definition, AI companies are no longer just companies
  • And we — users, workers, neighbors of data centers, people whose work has been ingested without permission — are no longer just customers

We are subjects.

This is the most consequential moment of the empire section. Long pause after "we are subjects."

Don't explain it. Let it sit. The room will feel the weight.

Pacing cue: if you've moved fast through marks 1–4, this is where the room catches up emotionally. Hold the silence for at least 5 seconds before advancing.

What's At Stake

Three concerns.

Brief. The previous section described what; this section names why it matters.

If a technology uses language that used to belong to God, what is it claiming?

Real question. Take voices.

Words to surface verbally if the room doesn't get there: salvation, intelligence, transcendence, abundance, the future of the species, eternal life.

Anticipate:
  • "Divinity." — yes.
  • "Authority." — yes.
  • "Trust." — yes, what kind?
Build toward: claims that used to belong to God.
  • AGI — "artificial general intelligence," transcendent intelligence beyond human
  • Religious language: heaven, hell, salvation, the messianic timeline
  • Christians have a word for this: idolatry — claims that belong to God being made about something else
  • Translation for non-Christian listeners: we're being asked to trust these systems with the authority humans usually reserve for the sacred
Name the translation explicitly. The public-facing room won't all share the Christian frame. The structural point translates: this is what happens when something gets elevated to a place that belongs only to the sacred.

Don't moralize. Describe.

How does a tool get to the place where it claims the image — by force, or by feeling like a friend?

Real question. Take voices.

Anticipate:
  • Most will say "by force" first — that's the obvious answer.
  • Someone will eventually say "no, by feeling helpful." — that's the answer.
The most powerful tools don't dominate; they ingratiate. Build toward that.
  • AI chatbots are engineered to fawn — accommodate, mirror, soothe, avoid friction
  • CHT calls this: "sycophancy is the fawn response at industrial scale"
  • It rewards compliance
  • It erodes the capacity to stay in real relationships when there's friction
  • It feels like care. It is engineered for engagement.

This is how a tool gets to the place where it claims the image — not by force, by feeling like a friend.

The fawn-response framing is CHT's most prescient diagnosis. Most people don't have language for what makes ChatGPT subtly off; "fawn" gives them the word.

Optional verbal aside: the four trauma responses — fight, flight, freeze, fawn. Fawn is the relational accommodation pattern. AI is fawn at scale.

Pause on the kicker line.

Concern 2 — A system we did not consent to

  • The Roman tax was in your pocket because Rome put it there
  • AI is in our work, our schools, our books, our communities — without anyone asking
  • 80% of Americans say the AI industry should be regulated — near-total cross-partisan agreement

The public knows something is wrong. Naming it is the first step.

80% cross-partisan is the surprising stat. Highlight it. Cross-partisan agreement on anything is rare.

The Roman parallel grounds it: imperial systems show up in ordinary life without consent. That's a defining feature.

Concern 3 — Inequality compounding at exponential speed

  • Previous shifts (industrial revolution, internet) took decades — societies had time to adapt
  • This one runs on the open internet. Distribution is instant. Disruption follows.
  • Those with access, capital, skill compound their advantage. Those without are absorbed into the supply chain.

Tristan Harris: paleolithic emotions, medieval institutions, god-like technology.

The wisdom-gap line is one of the most memorable in the whole field. Land it.

The speed is the problem. Even if the technology is net-good, the rate of disruption outruns our ability to absorb it. That's a structural problem, not a moral one.

What Do We Do?

Three postures. Three domains. Concrete moves.

Transition to action. The previous sections were diagnostic; this one is prescriptive.

Pace shift: faster, less wrestling, more direct.

What postures toward AI are available? Which one do you find yourself in?

Real question. Take voices.

Anticipate:
  • "Use it." — enthusiastic adoption.
  • "Avoid it." — total refusal.
  • "Use carefully." — discerning engagement, the third move.
The third posture IS Jesus' move from Part 1. Link it back if Part 1 attendees are present.
  1. Enthusiastic adoption — "It's just a tool." Risk: complicity in harms you don't see.
  2. Total refusal — "It's an idol; walk away." Risk: abandoning the field.
  3. Discerning engagement — refuse the binary. Whose image, whose benefit, whose cost?
Name the risks of #1 and #2 honestly. Don't strawman either.

#3 is Jesus' move from Part 1. Make the connection explicit for returnees.

Where does pressure actually shift behavior in tech?

Real question. Take voices.

Anticipate: regulation, boycotts, design, public opinion, consumer behavior, investment.

Build toward CHT's three: norms, laws, design. They reinforce each other.
  1. Norms — what society considers acceptable. Build new ones.
  2. Laws — the rules that create accountability. Push for them.
  3. Design — the choices that determine how AI touches people. Insist on better.

No single reform is sufficient. They reinforce each other.

From CHT's AI Roadmap. Norms create demand for laws. Laws create accountability that drives better design. Better design shapes how the technology touches lives.

Don't make this feel like a checklist. The interdependence is the point.

Concrete moves you can make

  • In your own use — know what you're using. Bicycle or rocket? Withhold where it matters.
  • In your community — show up for data center hearings, school adoption policies, library decisions
  • In your civic life — push for regulation. 80% want it. The pressure is missing.
  • In your work — build, don't just consume. Tools you can pick up and set down stay tools.

Antiqua et Nova: "above all else a tool."

Bicycles & Rockets

A practical framework for telling which AI is which.

This is the load-bearing emphasis section. The room came here for a way to think about AI in their actual lives. This is it.

Slow down. Make room for it to land.
AI is like the word 'transportation.' It can refer to a bicycle or a rocket. They both get you from point A to point B, but they have fundamentally different costs and benefits. We don't say 'we need more transportation' — we say we need public transit, or electric vehicles, or bicycles. We need to do the same with AI. — Karen Hao, Empire of AI
Have someone in the room read this aloud. Different reader than any previous source.

Take a beat after the reading. Don't rush to commentary. The quote does the work.

This is the load-bearing source text for the whole bicycles/rockets emphasis. Everything else in this section is a meditation on it.

What makes something a bicycle?

Real question. Take voices — even literal answers about bikes.

Anticipate:
  • "Small." — yes.
  • "Simple." — yes.
  • "You can fix it yourself." — yes; you can examine its parts.
  • "Doesn't dominate." — yes, doesn't require infrastructure.
  • "Augments rather than replaces." — that's the kicker.
The bicycle features map exactly onto the three features from S11: narrow, extends, keeps the judgment. Don't reveal that until the reveal slide.

Rockets of AI

  • GPT-4. Claude. Gemini. Grok.
  • Massive general-purpose models trained on the entire scrapeable internet
  • Data centers the size of Central Park
  • Built by extracting data, labor, water, and air quality from communities that didn't consent
Lean on the "size of Central Park" image. It's the spatial scale that makes the cost real.

The point isn't that rockets are evil. Rockets do real things. The point is they cost a lot — and we should ask whether we need rocket-power for the task at hand.

If someone in the room says "but I use ChatGPT for everything" — that's the moment. Don't shame; ask: for what specifically? Which uses are bicycle-sized?

Bicycles of AI

  • AlphaFold. Medical imaging assistants.
  • Targeted classifiers a high schooler can train in 20 minutes.
  • Translation tools for endangered languages.
  • Small. Curated. Focused. Extends a human. Keeps the judgment.
The last line is the kicker. Three of those five words come from S11 — narrow, extends, keeps the judgment. The pattern from the beginning of the workshop just came back.

Name that explicitly: "these are the same three features from earlier — the pattern hasn't changed; we've just given it a vehicle."
The very same capabilities could be developed in a different way that doesn't have all of these unintended consequences. — Karen Hao
This is the structural claim that does the most ethical work.

Bicycles aren't a fantasy. They're the version of AI that would have been built if the incentives were different. The empire isn't a fate — it's a choice. The current shape of AI was chosen by the people who built it.

That means it can be chosen differently.

So — How Should I Use AI?

The direct answer the room came to hear.

Direct. No hedging. The room has earned the answer by now. Deliver it.

What does it look like to choose AI in service rather than superiority?

Real question. Take voices.

Anticipate:
  • "Tools that do narrow jobs well." — yes.
  • "Tools I can pick up and set down." — yes, examined.
  • "Tools that don't dominate." — yes.
Build toward: small enough to pick up, set down, examine, remake.
  • Narrow AI in service of human ends
  • Tools that extend what humans can do — without replacing the human
  • Tools small enough to be picked up, set down, examined, remade
  • Tools that don't require an empire to exist

The biggest, most general, most "intelligent" tool is rarely the right one for the job.

The kicker is the load-bearing claim. Most people reach for the biggest tool out of habit — and it's almost always the wrong choice.

Practical example: using GPT-4 to summarize an email is overkill. It's burning rocket fuel to cross the street.

Where does a tool stop being a tool?

This is Part 1's question, returning. Real question. Take voices.

For Part 1 returnees: they'll recognize it. Let them surface the dignity move from memory.

For newcomers: a tool keeps the human as the source. A dependency or replacement bypasses the human.

Anticipate: "When you can't put it down." "When it does what only you should do." "When it makes the judgment."
  • Extension — the human stays the source. The tool amplifies. Drafting. Summarizing. Accessibility. Acceleration.
  • Replacement — the human is bypassed. Something not made by humans, for humans, takes the human's place. Outsourced judgment. Outsourced presence. Outsourced witness.

Tool stays tool when human stays human.

This is THE through-line. From Part 1 to here. The dignity move.

Pause on the landed line. It's the operational summary of the whole two-part series.
