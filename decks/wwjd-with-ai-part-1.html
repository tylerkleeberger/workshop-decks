What Would
Jesus Do (With AI)?

A discipleship conversation on technology,
empire, and the image we reflect.
SUNDAY · MAY 10, 2026 · 8:30 – 10:00 AM
Where we begin

"Is AI good or bad?"
might be the wrong question.

The better question:

How does Jesus help us think about a powerful, paradigm-shifting technology produced by some of the most powerful entities in modern society?

How have Christians dialogued and decided on approaches and ethics to technology throughout history?

Printing presses. Railroads. Automobiles. Television. The internet.

Each one reshaped daily life. Each one provoked the church to ask: what now?

Jesus didn't see the printing press, electricity, or the internet.

But he did live inside an imperial economy whose technologies reshaped daily life — often violently.

  • Roads
  • Coinage
  • Census
  • Taxation
  • Military supply chains
PART ONE

Mark 12

Jesus has just entered Jerusalem, cleansed the Temple, and openly confronted Israel's leadership. They send a coalition to trap him.

The trap

Pharisees + Herodians.

Two ends of the spectrum. They don't get along. Whatever he answers, one of them gets him.

MARK 12:12–15

Then the chief priests, the teachers of the law and the elders looked for a way to arrest him because they knew he had spoken the parable against them. But they were afraid of the crowd; so they left him and went away.

Later they sent some of the Pharisees and Herodians to Jesus to catch him in his words. They came to him and said, "Teacher, we know that you are a man of integrity. You aren't swayed by others, because you pay no attention to who they are; but you teach the way of God in accordance with the truth. Is it right to pay the imperial tax to Caesar or not? Should we pay or shouldn't we?"

The denarius is not just money.

It bears the image of Tiberius Caesar with the inscription:

"Tiberius Caesar, son of the Divine Augustus"
and on the reverse: "Pontifex Maximus" — high priest.

To a Jew, this coin is a portable idol.

A violation of the first and second commandments every time it changes hands.

Either answer kills him.

  1. Pay the tax. Affirm Roman occupation. Endorse imperial cult. Lose moral authority with the people.
  2. Don't pay the tax. Tax revolt. Sedition. Hand him to Rome.

There is no good answer.

MARK 12:16–17

But Jesus knew their hypocrisy. "Why are you trying to trap me?" he asked. "Bring me a denarius and let me look at it." They brought the coin, and he asked them, "Whose image is this? And whose inscription?"

"Caesar's," they replied.

Then Jesus said to them, "Give back to Caesar what is Caesar's and to God what is God's."

Why does Jesus ask them
to show him a coin?
Why does Jesus ask them
to show him a coin?
  • They have one. He doesn't.
  • They've already accommodated themselves to the imperial economy in a way he hasn't.
  • They are exposed before he answers.
What is Jesus' claim?
Layer 1 — the surface

"Whose image is this?"
"Caesar's."
"Then give it back to him."

Pay the tax. Caesar's coin can go back to Caesar. Done.

But this is a setup.

Layer 2 — the unspoken

The Greek word for "image" is εἰκών (eikōn).

Anyone listening hears the echo.

"…and give back to God what bears God's image."
Layer 3 — the apathy beat

What belongs to Caesar is small.

A coin. Fine. Let him have it.

What belongs to God is everything else.

Caesar gets a coin. God gets the world and everyone in it.

This is why they walked away amazed.

He didn't escape the trap.

He dissolved it.

Jesus diminishes the tax question and elevates the human one.

PART TWO

The Question Travels

How do we use Jesus' approach for thinking and navigating our current landscape?

The denarius traveled into every Galilean home, carrying with it a religious claim.

"Tiberius Caesar, son of the divine Augustus, high priest."

You couldn't buy bread without participating in that claim.

The smartphone, the chatbot, the AI assistant carry a related but sharper claim.

What is being asked of us:

  • Ownership — give us your data, your work, your attention
  • Subservience — accept the terms; there is no negotiation
  • Access — let us mediate what you read, who you talk to, what you know
  • Dependency — let us become the thing you can't think without

Language that used to belong to God: salvation, intelligence, transcendence, abundance, the future of the species.

The test:
Whose image are you carrying back at the end?

Tool vs. dependency.

Tool vs. exploitation.

Which dream is being prioritized?

The line is the moment a tool stops being a tool.

Something elevated to the place that belongs to God.

Caesar reaching past the coin to claim the image.

PART THREE

The Practice

Three habits for approaching AI like Jesus.
Habit 1

The question on the way in.

Before you pick up a new tool — before you let one into your house, your work, your kid's life — ask:

Whose image is on this?
Whose benefit?
Whose cost?

Not paranoia. Discernment.

Habit 2

The audit on the way through.

Tools made by humans, used by humans, can extend the work of image-bearers.

The trouble starts when the tool starts producing what only image-bearers were made to produce — judgment, witness, presence, love.

Extension: the human stays the source. The tool amplifies.

Replacement: the human is bypassed. Something not made in God's image takes the human's place.

Tool stays a tool when human stays human.

Habit 3

The line you don't cross.

The line is directional, not a checklist.

Two kingdoms are being served:

  • God's kingdom — extension toward neighbor, dignity, image, presence
  • Caesar's kingdom — extraction, replacement, dependency, the diminishment of the human

The line is anything that crosses from the first into the second.

PART FOUR

So — do I use AI
or not?

A direct answer.

Yes — often. With these specific habits.

  • Use what extends.
  • Refuse what replaces.
  • Push for what serves.

The question to take with you:

What world is being created
by the things I carry?

How do the tools I use participate
in the world —
and whose world is it claiming?

For us, then:

  • Whose image is on you?
  • Who do you serve?
  • What kingdom are you participating in — and how do you know?
  • What do you hold that cannot be rendered unto Caesar?
  • What can be a tool that genuinely enhances God's dream for the world?
